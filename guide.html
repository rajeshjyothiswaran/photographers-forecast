Photographer's Forecast — User Guide

A planning tool for golden hour color, fog, stars, dark skies & tides · Built for LadyBugFarm Images

What this tool does

Photographer's Forecast pulls 7-day weather model data and scores each day on four things photographers care about: sunrise color potential, sunset color potential, clear skies for stars, and fog formation. Scores run from 0–100 — they are not percentages. Think of them as a confidence rating: 80+ means conditions are strongly aligned, 30 and below means it's probably not worth the drive.

For coastal locations, a tide chart automatically appears at the bottom of the detail panel showing the full day's tide curve, high/low times, and approximate sunrise/sunset markers — so you can plan your shoot around both light and water level.

It does not replace checking conditions the evening before. Use it to identify the days worth watching, then confirm closer to the time.

The four forecast tabs

🌅 Sunrise color

Scores the probability of vivid color during the golden hour window (45 min before to 75 min after sunrise). The key factors are low cloud coverage between 20–75% (broken clouds that catch light from below), humidity, and mid/high cloud structure. A completely clear sky scores low — there's nothing to paint. A fully overcast sky also scores low — the light can't get through.

The detail panel shows exact sunrise time, the scoring window, blue hour and golden hour times, and moonrise for reference.

The "Smoulder zone" badge means low cloud coverage is in the 40–75% sweet spot — the classic condition for vivid orange skies on the Sonoma Coast.

🌇 Sunset color

Same logic as sunrise, applied to the sunset window (90 min before to 45 min after sunset). Coastal locations get a bonus for the marine stratus pattern — broken low cloud over the Pacific at sunset is one of the most reliable color producers on this coastline.

The "Painter's sky" badge marks the premium pattern: dense high cirrus (which diffuses warm light across the whole sky) combined with broken low clouds that catch fire from below.

★ Stars

Scores astrophotography conditions for the night following the selected day. The score reflects cloud cover in the best 4-hour stretch of the dark window, moon phase and illumination, moon-free hours, humidity, and light pollution (Bortle scale). A full moon will crush the score regardless of sky clarity.

The Milky Way core window shows the hours when the galactic center is above 10° and — critically — whether the moon is up during that window. "Moon-lit" means the core is geometrically visible but the moon prevents dark-sky shooting. "Moon-free" means a genuine window exists.

Preset locations have hand-coded Bortle values. Salt Point SP is Bortle 2 (genuinely dark). Custom searched locations default to Bortle 5 — treat those estimates cautiously.

☁ Fog

Scores the probability of radiation fog or marine stratus between 3–10 AM. The primary drivers are the dewpoint-temperature spread (below 3°C/5°F strongly favors fog), humidity above 80%, and calm winds. Coastal locations get a bonus for the marine layer pattern.

If you're at a ridge location (like Mercuryville), a high fog score means a sea-of-clouds opportunity below you — not fog at your feet. The detail panel and verdict text will note this explicitly.

🌊 Tide chart

How it works

For any coastal location — whether a preset, a saved spot, or a city you've searched — a tide chart automatically appears at the bottom of the detail panel. The tool finds the nearest tide station and shows the full tide curve for that day, with high/low markers and approximate sunrise and sunset times overlaid.

This lets you answer the most important coastal planning question at a glance: what is the tide doing during golden hour?

Reading the chart

The curve shows water height in feet (MLLW datum) across a full 24-hour day. Green dots mark high tides, orange dots mark low tides. The 🌅 and 🌇 dashed lines mark approximate sunrise and sunset times so you can see exactly where the tide is during your shooting window.

Below the chart, a table lists each high and low with its exact time and height. Heights can go slightly negative — that means the water is below the average low water line, which often means bonus beach or reef exposure.

Data sources

US locations use NOAA Tides & Currents — the official US government harmonic tide prediction service. Free and highly accurate. The station name and data source are shown below the chart.

International locations use TideCheck, which covers 20,000+ stations across 200+ countries using NOAA, TICON-4, and FES2022 data. The chart footer will show "TideCheck" for these locations.

Tides are only shown when the nearest station is within 60km. For inland locations like Geyserville or Mercuryville, the tide section is silently hidden — it's not relevant there.

Planning for shore photography

Low tide at golden hour is usually the most interesting — more beach, more rocks, tide pools exposed, and reflective wet sand. A falling tide during golden hour is often better than a rising one as it leaves wet reflective surfaces. Extremely low tides (negative values) expose formations that are normally underwater.

High tide at sunset can be dramatic for wave action against cliffs or rocks, but reduces foreground options significantly.

My Locations

All your shooting locations live in one unified My Locations section at the top of the page. Four default spots are pre-loaded (Jenner, Salt Point SP, Mercuryville, Geyserville) but every location is editable — you can remove any of the defaults and replace them with your own.

To add a location: search any city, zip code, or place name → select from the dropdown → click Save to My Locations. Saved locations persist across sessions in your browser.

To remove a location: hover over any card and click the ✕ that appears in the top right corner.

How to use it — a practical workflow

1
Pick your location. Choose from My Locations, search any city, zip code, or place worldwide, or enter lat/lon manually. Your locations and the last-viewed location persist across sessions.
2
Scan the 7-day cards. The bars and scores give you an at-a-glance picture of the week. Look for the tab you care about highlighted in green. The active tab is shown at full opacity; others are dimmed.
3
Tap a day to read the detail panel. Check the verdict text — it describes the specific sky pattern, not just a score tier. Check the metric cards for exact times and cloud layer breakdown.
4
Check the tide chart for coastal locations. Scroll to the bottom of the detail panel. See where the tide sits during your shooting window, whether it's rising or falling, and how exposed the beach will be at golden hour.
5
Expand the hourly cloud detail. The collapsible table shows cloud cover hour-by-hour around the golden hour window. Rows highlighted in blue are inside the scoring window. This is where you spot last-minute shifts.
6
Check again the evening before. Model accuracy drops beyond 3–4 days. Always recheck the night before a shoot, especially for fog which can form or dissipate within hours.
7
Report back. Use the "Report what you actually saw" button at the bottom of any detail panel to send feedback. This helps improve the scoring over time.

Key terms explained

D-P spread Dewpoint-temperature spread. When this is below 3°F (1.7°C), the air is nearly saturated and fog is likely. Above 10°F means dry air and no fog. The single most useful fog predictor.
Score window The exact time range used to calculate the sunrise or sunset score. Sunrise: 45 min before to 75 min after. Sunset: 90 min before to 45 min after.
Blue hour The ~30 minutes before sunrise or after sunset when the sky is deeply blue and light is soft and even. Excellent for seascape and landscape work that doesn't need golden light.
Golden hour The ~54 minutes after sunrise or before sunset when the sun is low and light is warm and directional. The primary scoring target for color potential.
Bortle scale A 1–9 measure of night sky darkness. Bortle 1 is pristine dark sky (rare). Bortle 9 is inner-city sky. For Milky Way core photography, Bortle 4 or below is desirable. Salt Point SP (Bortle 2) and Mercuryville (Bortle 3) are your best options in this region.
MW core window The hours when the galactic center (Sagittarius direction) is above 10° altitude at your location AND it's dark enough to shoot. Intersected with the moon-free window to show actual usable hours.
Smoulder zone 40–75% low cloud coverage — the sweet spot where clouds are broken enough to let light through but dense enough to catch and scatter color. The best badge to see on a sunrise or sunset forecast.
AQI badge Air Quality Index. When wildfire smoke is present, a low AQI (under 50) may actually enhance warm tones. Above 100 starts to degrade color saturation and visibility significantly.
MLLW datum Mean Lower Low Water — the reference level used for tide heights. Zero means average low water. Negative values mean the tide is below average low water, which exposes more beach and reef than usual.

Tips from the field

💡
A 65 with "Smoulder zone" beats an 80 with "Clear sky." Cloud pattern matters more than the combined score for color. A broken marine layer at the coast at sunset is more reliable than a theoretically high-scoring clear day.
💡
For fog, arrive 30–45 minutes before the window opens. Radiation fog peaks around first light and burns off fast. Arriving at 7 AM when the fog window was 4–7 AM means you probably missed it.
💡
Mercuryville on a high fog day is a sea-of-clouds shoot. You're at 2,720 ft — above the inversion layer. Check both the fog score (is the sea of clouds there?) and the sunrise score (will the light be good on top of it?).
💡
For stars, dark window length matters as much as the score. A 6h moon-free window scoring 50 is often more useful than a 1.5h window scoring 75. Check the "Dark window" metric card, not just the headline number.
💡
Low tide + golden hour is the sweet spot for shore photography. Check the tide chart for the day you're planning. A falling tide during the golden hour window leaves wet reflective sand and exposes more foreground rocks and pools.
💡
Negative tides unlock locations. When the chart shows values below 0 ft, formations that are normally submerged become accessible. These are often the best mornings for tide pool and reef photography.
💡
Light smoke can enhance color. AQI 50–80 adds warm atmospheric scattering that deepens reds at golden hour. Above 100 degrades color and visibility significantly. The smoke note in the detail panel flags both cases.
💡
The hourly cloud table is your last-minute check. Expand it the night before. The ★-marked rows are inside the golden hour window. Low clouds trending from 60% down to 20% through the window is a clearing pattern — often better than the daily average suggests.

Limitations & honest caveats

Scores are estimates, not guarantees. They are based on weather model output which has known limitations — especially for fog, which can form or dissipate within an hour based on local terrain effects no model captures well. Always verify conditions yourself before committing to a long drive.

Forecast accuracy degrades beyond 3–4 days. Days 5–7 are useful for spotting trends and blocking your calendar, but treat them as rough guidance rather than reliable predictions.

Tide predictions are for planning only. Tide data comes from harmonic models and is not suitable for navigation or safety decisions. Always check official local sources before entering the water or accessing coastal areas.

Milky Way core visibility is geometry + weather combined. The tool calculates when the galactic center is above 10° altitude and intersects that with the dark and moon-free window. It does not account for horizon obstructions like mountains or trees at your specific shooting spot.

Light pollution for custom locations is estimated. Preset locations have hand-verified Bortle values. Any location you search defaults to Bortle 5 (suburban sky). For accurate light pollution at a custom location, cross-reference with lightpollutionmap.info.

AQI data may not always load. Air quality comes from a separate API and is optional — if it fails, the forecast still works normally without smoke badges.

Data sources

Weather Open-Meteo — free, no API key. 7-day hourly forecast including cloud layers, temperature, dewpoint, humidity, wind, and precipitation.
Air quality Open-Meteo Air Quality API — US AQI forecast. Optional — the tool works without it.
Geocoding OpenStreetMap Nominatim — powers the city search. © OpenStreetMap contributors.
US tides NOAA Tides & Currents — official US harmonic tide predictions. Free, no API key required. Used for all US coastal locations within 60km of a NOAA station.
Intl tides TideCheck — global tide predictions for 20,000+ stations in 200+ countries. Used for international coastal locations outside NOAA coverage. Data sourced from TICON-4 (CC-BY-4.0) and FES2022.
Sun & moon SunCalc (inline, adapted from Vladimir Agafonkin's BSD-licensed library) — sunrise, sunset, astronomical twilight, moon phase, moonrise and moonset.
Milky Way Computed from first principles using galactic core RA/Dec and local sidereal time. No external API.
